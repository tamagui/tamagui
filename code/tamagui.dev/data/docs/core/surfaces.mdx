---
title: Surfaces and levels
description: One operation — re-binding theme generics against the color scale, scoped to a subtree
---

Panels, wells, toolbars, per-area restyling, and whole-surface press states are
all one operation: re-binding the generic theme keys against the color scale,
scoped to a subtree. v3 ships two entry points for that operation and adds one
convention on top. There is no separate surface engine.

## Two vocabulary layers

Keep raw material and styling keys separate:

- **Scales** (`color1`..`color12` per scheme) are raw material.
- **Generics** (`background`, `backgroundHover`, `backgroundPress`, `borderColor`,
  `color`, …) are the only keys a skin may style against.

A skin never references `$color9` directly. This one rule is what makes everything
below compose: anything that re-binds the generics restyles every skin beneath it,
with no skin cooperation needed.

## Levels are sub-themes

`surface1`, `surface2`, and `surface3` are sub-themes that re-bind the generics a
little darker (or lighter) than their parent. "Everything in this panel a step up"
is just `theme="surface2"`:

```tsx
<Theme name="surface2">
  <YStack backgroundColor="$background" borderColor="$borderColor">
    {/* every skin in here reads the surface2 generics */}
  </YStack>
</Theme>
```

The light and dark asymmetry (light hover shifts up the scale, dark shifts down)
lives in the theme templates and is resolved once at theme-build time, not in
per-component logic. Custom levels are a template you define; the builder
generates the light and dark variants. They are named, typed, and class-swap
cheap on the web.

## Surface is the panel fixture

For layout code — panels, wells, toolbars — copy the [Surface](/ui/surface)
fixture. It is a `YStack` + a `level` prop + the composable facets, with nothing
on by default:

```tsx
<Surface level={2} filled outlined rounded interactive />
```

Because the facets read generics and `level` re-binds those generics, a level
restyles every facet with zero cooperation. Component skins (Card, the Select
popover, ListItem) do not extend Surface — they get their family resemblance by
styling against the same generics and setting their own level.

## Variables is the one-off escape hatch

When a patch lives in exactly one place, use
[`Variables`](/docs/core/variables) instead of naming a sub-theme:

```tsx
<Variables values={{ backgroundHover: '$blue4' }}>
  {/* this panel's hover is blue4; everything else inherits */}
</Variables>
```

The rule: used in more than one place, make it a sub-theme; used once,
`Variables`. Same operation, two entry points, chosen by whether it deserves a
name.

## Custom knobs go in config.variables

Numbers that should cascade but are not part of the color palette —
`$radius`, `$pressScale`, `$focusRingWidth`, `$disabledOpacity`, easings — are
declared once in `config.variables`, typed through module augmentation, and read
by skins the same way generics are. The facets read `$radius` and `$pressScale`
from here. This is also how you ship your own tokens.

Do not reintroduce component-scoped tokens like `$surfaceBorder`. Inside a level
the border already is `$borderColor`; the template re-bound it.

## What to learn, in total

- Style skins against generics, never the scale.
- `theme="surface1-3"` shifts a subtree's level.
- `Variables` patches one-offs.
- `Surface` is a copy-paste panel primitive.
- Custom knobs go in `config.variables`.

## Not rebuilt on purpose

Whole-subtree state re-theming — a parent press recoloring its children — is
deliberately not rebuilt; that was v2 component-theme territory, expensive and
confusing. Where a real case exists (list selection), the state vocabulary emits
`data-state` and group styles cover it. Relative levels (`level="+1"` compounding
under nesting) are also deferred: theme resolution is absolute today, and named
absolute levels cover the cases seen so far.
